Introduction

Access to healthy, affordable, and culturally appropriate food is a fundamental component of community well-being. This comprehensive analysis examines the state of food access in Vallejo, California, with particular attention to disparities between neighborhoods of different socioeconomic status. The research combines community focus groups with 33 participants and a detailed survey of 70 food stores throughout the city.

Key Context

Vallejo has a higher than average population of people of color and demonstrates significant challenges in food access that disproportionately affect low-income neighborhoods. These challenges represent a form of environmental inequity that impacts the health and well-being of residents.

The study specifically focuses on access to fruits, vegetables, and non-dairy/meat alternatives, excluding comments related to dairy products and meat to maintain focus on healthy food alternatives.

Barriers to Accessing Healthy Food

Through focus groups with 33 Vallejo residents, researchers identified six major barriers to accessing healthy food in the community. These barriers interact with each other, compounding the challenges faced by residents, particularly those in low-income neighborhoods.

Store Location & Transportation

Residents described significant challenges to purchasing food due to the inconvenient location of stores in their neighborhoods. The geographic barriers include:

  • Food stores located too far from residences
  • Neighborhoods without adequate sidewalk infrastructure
  • Unsafe streets due to traffic conditions
  • Stores located in hills or different communities

These location issues force residents to either shop at convenience stores or patronize shops with fewer food options. Transportation limitations further exacerbate the problem:

"I personally think they're [stores] too far to walk, especially."
"Riding the bus, I can only have one batch as I have a purse. Usually the bus doesn't stop right in front of you, kind of down a block. So, there's a walking distance you have to walk through like 2–3 streets away."

The difficulty of transporting groceries without a car limits the amount of food that can be purchased at once and necessitates more frequent shopping trips:

"… Maria, she has a cart. And then when I have my grandkids, I have a stroller and I put everything in the stroller and sometimes I have to not buy that much and just get what I need for the day. So you know, not that much."

Affordability

Cost represents a significant barrier to accessing healthy food in Vallejo. Several factors contribute to this issue:

  • High standard cost of living in the Bay Area
  • Limited shopping options leading to reliance on convenience stores with higher prices
  • Small, local markets charging premium prices
"[Small, local grocery stores] are my closest stores; we only do small shopping there as things are pricier."
"Oftentimes we don't buy specific (healthy) foods because of its cost."

The combination of transportation limitations and lack of full-service grocery stores in certain neighborhoods forces many residents to rely on more expensive local shops:

"Both (smaller, local stores) have mainly everything but like I said less quality for a higher price."

The price premium for organic options presents an additional barrier for those seeking healthier options:

"Especially when it is organic. It is usually more expensive. So I usually buy the less expensive ones 'cause it is cheaper."

Lack of Full-Service Grocery Stores

Across Vallejo, residents reported a scarcity of options for purchasing food. The limited full-service grocery stores include:

  • Grocery Outlet
  • The Dollar Store
  • 99 Cents Only Store
  • Smart & Final
  • Safeway (often noted as too expensive)

In addition to these stores, residents purchase food at small local businesses, though these often come with higher prices and limited selection.

The loss of key grocery stores, such as Raley's, has further limited access to quality food options:

"This town should have a health food store and organic market like Marin County where I'm from, but I don't see any other place here where I can pick up that kind of food. Raley's is going out (of business), you know, they had real good quality stuff there."

Lack of Access Leads to Adapting Meals

The limited availability of ingredients forces residents to adapt their meal planning and cooking. Participants described having to:

  • Change planned meals due to missing ingredients
  • Visit multiple stores to gather all needed items
  • Abandon certain recipes due to the effort required to source ingredients
"Disappointed, if I'm wanting to make something or do something and I can't make it then I'm disappointed."
"If I have to cook a meal, and then, man, I have to go to Food Max for this and then the Filipino place for that, do I give up that meal? Sometimes I do; it's not worth it for me to do all that."

Most focus group participants discussed visiting multiple stores to acquire the food they need, sharing tips on where to find the cheapest and best quality produce and food products, including cauliflower, peppers, green beans, avocados, and tortillas.

Poor Food Quality

Stores that sell food but are not full-service grocery stores frequently have food options of poor quality, making them less desirable for cooking. Quality issues include:

  • Rotten or spoiled produce
  • Expired food products
  • Deceptive practices (moving expired products to the front)
"Yeah, old burritos, yeah, they put the new ones in the back and the old ones in the front."
"The thing is, though, you have to eat it (the food) right away, the 99 cent store, you cannot store it (the food); it will go bad."

These quality issues create frustration and mistrust toward local food retailers and further limit viable food options for residents.

Problems with Donated Foods

While many participants expressed appreciation for local food banks that increase access to affordable foods, the quality of donated items was frequently problematic:

  • Bread and vegetables that were rotten or moldy
  • Substandard food through church donation programs
  • Expired canned food products

The Food Empowerment Project (F.E.P.) expressed particular concern that organizations professing to provide for the nutritional needs of low-income and elderly people are in some cases providing food that could worsen their situation. Though beyond the scope of this study, the practices of food banks and other food donation centers deserve further scrutiny.

Survey Results

To complement the qualitative focus group data, researchers conducted a comprehensive survey of 70 food retail establishments throughout Vallejo. The findings highlight systemic issues in food access that disproportionately impact low-income neighborhoods.

Types of Stores

The distribution of store types varies significantly between high-income and low-income neighborhoods, with concerning implications for food access:

Low-Income Neighborhoods

  • Grocery Stores: 12%
  • Convenience Stores: 46%
  • Liquor Stores: 29%
  • Specialty Stores: 13%

Total: 48 stores

High-Income Neighborhoods

  • Grocery Stores: 36%
  • Convenience Stores: 41%
  • Liquor Stores: 9%
  • Specialty Stores: 14%

Total: 22 stores

Key Finding

Eighty-eight percent of all liquor stores and 71% of all convenience stores in Vallejo are located in low-income neighborhoods. There is a liquor store for every 2,769 residents in low-income areas, compared to every 51,041 residents in high-income neighborhoods.

In low-income neighborhoods, three-quarters (75.5%) of all stores are liquor stores, corner stores, or convenience stores. Of these 35 stores, about one-third sell no produce or fewer than 5 types of produce. Sixty percent of those stores that do sell produce provide all or almost all of it only in cans. However, 91% of these stores sell beer, wine and/or liquor.

Produce Availability

The study recorded the availability of 119 types of fruits and vegetables in fresh, frozen, and canned/jarred versions across the surveyed stores. Key findings include:

  • In high-income areas, about 22.7% of stores had fewer than 10 types of produce available in any version
  • In low-income neighborhoods, 50% of stores had fewer than 10 types of produce

This disparity highlights that despite having more stores numerically (48 compared to 22), low-income neighborhoods have reduced access to produce variety because the additional stores tend to be liquor, corner, and convenience stores with limited fresh food options.

Store Grading Methodology

Each store received a grade based on five criteria:

  1. Variety of produce available
  2. Amount of produce available in fresh versions
  3. Amount of produce available in organic versions
  4. Amount of produce available only in conventional versions
  5. Amount of produce available only in canned/jarred versions

Grades ranged from A to F, with higher grades indicating better overall access and quality.

Store Grading Results

No stores in Vallejo received an "A" grade. The stores with the best grades (B) tended to be grocery stores located in higher-income areas. In low-income areas, stores with a middling grade of C were primarily grocery stores clustered in the central-northern part of Vallejo, leaving many in the southern and southwestern neighborhoods without easy access to a grocery store.

Fresh Produce Access

Fresh produce is particularly important for healthy meal preparation and snacking. The survey found significant barriers to accessing fresh produce in Vallejo:

  • Over 25% of stores in both high and low-income neighborhoods have no fresh produce available
  • Only 10 stores throughout Vallejo have salad bars
  • While 40.9% of stores in high-income areas promote fresh produce through endcaps and special displays, only 16.7% of stores in low-income neighborhoods do the same

Cleanliness Concerns

Over 50% of stores in low-income neighborhoods were evaluated as not providing "a clean and sanitary environment for fresh fruits and vegetables," further limiting the appeal and safety of available produce.

The combination of limited availability, poor quality, and sanitation concerns creates significant challenges for residents attempting to maintain healthy diets rich in fresh fruits and vegetables.

Environmental Justice & Equity

Environmental Justice/Environmental Racism

The food access challenges in Vallejo represent a form of environmental injustice that disproportionately affects communities of color and low-income residents.

Defining Environmental Justice

In 1991, the First National People of Color Environmental Leadership Summit drafted and adopted 17 principles of environmental justice. In essence, environmental justice encompasses where "we live, work and play, go to school (and sometimes it includes to pray)."

Environmental racism includes both the intended and unintended consequences of environmental rules and regulations, which are often lax or unenforced, directly impacting people of color.

Food Access as Environmental Racism

The lack of healthy foods in communities of color and low-income communities is something that the Food Empowerment Project views as a form of environmental racism. Simple terms such as "food deserts" and "food security issues" should not mask the huge problem that is the reality for many of those living in the United States.

Health of Communities of Color in California

Lack of Access to Healthy Foods

The United States Department of Agriculture defines food deserts as:

"[U]rban neighborhoods and rural towns without ready access to fresh, healthy, and affordable food. Instead of supermarkets and grocery stores, these communities may have no food access or are served only by fast food restaurants and convenience stores that offer few healthy, affordable food options."

However, this definition has limitations. The Food Empowerment Project notes that this is a narrow view of the problem that doesn't account for:

  • People who are time-poor as well as cash-poor
  • The significant barrier that cost presents
  • Cultural appropriateness of available foods

A more comprehensive understanding recognizes that adequate access to food means that nutritious and culturally appropriate foods must be available on a consistent basis, at an affordable price, and within a reasonable distance among communities.

Conclusion & Recommendations

The comprehensive analysis of food access in Vallejo reveals systemic challenges that disproportionately affect low-income neighborhoods and communities of color. These issues represent a form of environmental injustice that impacts the health and well-being of residents.
